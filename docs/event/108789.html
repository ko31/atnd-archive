Solar panel systems are great — but once the Sun goes down, they change into expensive slabs of sheet metal.

But a new invention could allow us to remain generating renewable energy even in the dark, the New York Situations reports. Electrical engineer Aaswath Raman, with the University of California with LA, has come up which includes a device that can harness energy from a dark night sky to power an LED — hinting in the new frontier in fast growing energy.
Raman’s findings were published while in the journal Joule today. His device — created from easy-to-find materials including Styrofoam as well as off-the-shelf aluminum parts — takes good thing about radiative cooling, the process which allows objects to release heat following a Sun sets.
The top portion from the device cools down quicker than the bottom, allowing it to turn the temperature swing into electricity by using a thermoelectric generator.
Regrettably, it’s not very potent. The prototype was able to power a white BROUGHT, but was still a few orders of magnitude less powerful over a solar panel per rectangular meter, as the Periods points out.
Unfortunately, it’s not necessarily very powerful. The prototype managed to power a whitened LED, but was still several orders of magnitude less powerful over a solar panel per square meter, as the Moments points out.
